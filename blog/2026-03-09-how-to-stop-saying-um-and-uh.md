You're presenting to your team, pitching an idea to your manager, or interviewing for a new role. Everything is going well — until you notice yourself saying *"um… so basically… uh… you know?"* seven times in two minutes.

Filler words leak authority. Research from the [Journal of Cognitive Psychology](https://www.tandfonline.com/) shows that listeners rate speakers with frequent filler words as less competent and less confident — even when the content of what they're saying is identical.

The good news? "Um" and "uh" are habits. And habits can be retrained.

---

## Why Do We Say "Um" and "Uh" in the First Place?

Filler words serve a purpose. Linguists call them *"hesitation phenomena"* — they're pause-fillers your brain uses while it's searching for the next word.

The problem is that the habit gets over-trained. You start using fillers not just when you're searching for a word, but as a default gap-filler between *every* sentence.

Three root causes:
1. **Speaking faster than you think.** You start a sentence before you've planned it.
2. **Fear of silence.** You fill pauses because silence feels awkward or weak.
3. **Lack of awareness.** Most people don't even realize how often they use fillers.

The fix addresses all three.

---

## Step 1: Diagnose Your Filler Habit

You cannot fix what you cannot measure. The first step is brutal self-honesty.

**How to do it:**
- Record yourself speaking for 2–3 minutes on any topic (your job, a recent project, a trip you took).
- Play it back and count every "um," "uh," "like," "you know," and "basically."
- Note *when* they cluster: at the start of sentences? When transitioning topics? When you don't know the answer?

Patterns reveal the trigger. Once you know your trigger, you can catch it in real time.

## Step 2: Train Yourself to Pause Instead

The most powerful replacement for "um" is **silence**.

A one-second pause sounds confident. It signals that you're choosing your words carefully. It gives your listener time to process. But it *feels* like an eternity when you're speaking.

**The pause drill:**
1. Pick a topic and set a timer for 3 minutes.
2. Speak out loud — but every time you feel the urge to say "um" or "uh," *stop completely*.
3. Hold the silence for a full beat. Then continue.

This is uncomfortable at first. Do it anyway. The discomfort fades. The habit rewires.

---

## Step 3: Slow Your Speaking Rate

Filler words thrive at high speaking speeds. When you talk fast, your mouth outruns your brain and reaches for fillers to bridge the gap.

Target speaking rate: **130–150 words per minute** for presentations. This feels slow in your head. It sounds natural to your audience.

**The 80% rule:** Speak at 80% of the speed that feels comfortable. Your audience gets clarity. You get time to think.

---

## Step 4: Build the "Sentence Blueprint" Habit

Most filler words appear at the *start* of sentences — because that's where your brain is deciding what to say.

Train yourself to blueprint before you speak:
- Think the first 5–7 words of your sentence before you say them out loud.
- Begin speaking only when you have that opening locked.

This small shift eliminates the most common filler word trigger: the stuttering start.

---

## Step 5: Daily Practice with Immediate Feedback

Reading a guide doesn't rewire a habit. **Daily repetition with immediate feedback does.**

This is where most people fail. They become aware of the problem but don't practice consistently enough to change the default behavior.

**Minimum effective dose:** 10 minutes of deliberate speaking practice per day for 21 consecutive days.

How to structure your practice:
- **Day 1–7:** Record yourself on casual topics (what you did today, a book you read). Count fillers manually or with AI.
- **Day 8–14:** Add professional topics (your role, a project update, a pitch). Focus on pause replacement.
- **Day 15–21:** Simulate high-pressure scenarios — pretend you're pitching to your CEO or answering an unexpected question in a meeting.

## Step 6: The "Accountability Listener" Technique

For faster results, add a social layer. Tell one person — a friend, spouse, or colleague — about your filler word goal. Ask them to signal you (a subtle hand gesture, a tap on the table) whenever they hear one.

This adds real-time conscious awareness in social and professional contexts, where the habit is hardest to break.

## Common Mistakes That Keep Filler Words Locked In

**Trying to suppress vs. replace:** Don't tell yourself "don't say um." Tell yourself "pause instead." The brain responds better to action cues than inhibition cues.

**Practicing only when alone:** You need to practice in realistic conditions — video calls, small groups, impromptu updates. Controlled practice alone isn't enough.

**Expecting overnight change:** Filler words are deep speech habits. Expect 3–4 weeks of consistent work before you notice a meaningful shift in high-pressure situations.
