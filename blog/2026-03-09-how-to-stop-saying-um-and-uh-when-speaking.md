# How to Stop Saying Um and Uh When Speaking (5 Proven Techniques)

If you've ever listened to a recording of yourself and cringed at how many times you said "um," "uh," "like," or "you know" — you're not alone. Filler words are one of the most common communication habits, and one of the most fixable.

The good news: stopping filler words doesn't require talent. It requires awareness, practice, and a simple system. Here are five techniques that actually work.

---

## Why Do We Say Um and Uh in the First Place?

Filler words are cognitive placeholders. Your brain uses them to buy time while it searches for the next word. They're not a sign of low intelligence — they're a sign that your mouth is moving faster than your thoughts.

The problem is perception. Listeners interpret heavy filler word use as uncertainty, lack of preparation, or low confidence — even when none of that is true. In job interviews, presentations, and leadership conversations, filler words create a credibility gap between how capable you are and how capable you *sound*.

---

## Technique 1: Record and Count

You can't fix what you can't see. The first step is brutal honesty: record yourself speaking for two minutes on any topic, then play it back and count every filler word.

Most people are shocked. The number is always higher than they expect. That shock is actually useful — it creates the motivation to change.

Once you have a baseline count, you have something concrete to improve. Aim to cut your count by 20% each week.

**Practical drill:** Use your phone's voice memo app. Record yourself explaining what you did at work today. Count the fillers. Write the number down.

---

## Technique 2: Embrace the Pause

The single most powerful replacement for "um" is silence.

When you feel a filler word coming — pause instead. A one-second silence feels like an eternity to you but is completely invisible to your audience. What listeners actually notice is that you seem thoughtful, deliberate, and in control.

Confident speakers pause. Nervous speakers fill.

Train yourself by reading a passage aloud and replacing every point where you'd normally say "um" with a clean two-second pause. Do this for five minutes a day and you'll rewire the habit within two weeks.

---

## Technique 3: Slow Down Before You Speed Up

Filler words multiply when you speak too fast. Your mouth races ahead of your thoughts and plugs the gaps with "uh."

Deliberately slowing your pace gives your brain time to form complete sentences before you say them. You won't need fillers because you'll already know what's coming next.

This feels unnatural at first. Most people think slow speech sounds boring. The reality is that measured, deliberate pacing sounds authoritative — think great TED talks, not fast-talking salespeople.

**Practical drill:** Speak at 60-70% of your normal speed for an entire practice session. It will feel wrong. It will sound right.

---

## Technique 4: Use Transitional Phrases Instead

Replace filler words with intentional transitional phrases that serve an actual function:

| Instead of... | Try... |
|--------------|--------|
| "Um, so..." | "Here's what I mean..." |
| "Uh, basically..." | "The key point is..." |
| "Like, you know..." | "To put it simply..." |
| "Um, I think..." | "My take is..." |

These phrases still give your brain a brief moment to catch up, but they signal structure and confidence rather than hesitation.

## How Long Does It Take to Stop Saying Um?

Most people see meaningful improvement in two to four weeks with daily practice. Complete elimination is rare and arguably unnecessary — one or two fillers per minute is within the normal range for even professional speakers.

The goal isn't perfection. The goal is control: knowing that you *can* pause instead of filling, and choosing to do it consistently when it matters.
