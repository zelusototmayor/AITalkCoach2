# How to Stop Saying Um and Uh When Speaking (5 Proven Techniques)

Filler words like "um" and "uh" creep into almost every conversation. Whether you're pitching an idea, presenting to a board, or answering an interview question, those little sounds can chip away at your credibility — even when your content is solid. The good news: learning how to stop saying um and uh when speaking is a skill, not a personality trait. With the right techniques and deliberate practice, you can clean up your speech faster than you think.

---

## Why Do We Say "Um" and "Uh" in the First Place?

Before you can fix the habit, it helps to understand where it comes from.

"Um" and "uh" are **hesitation fillers** — acoustic placeholders your brain uses while it searches for the next word or thought. Linguists call them "filled pauses." They signal to listeners that you're still talking and haven't finished your thought, which is actually a useful social function in casual conversation.

The problem is context. In professional settings — a job interview, a sales call, a keynote — filled pauses read as uncertainty or lack of preparation. Research published in the *Journal of Cognitive Psychology* found that listeners consistently rate speakers with fewer filler words as more competent and trustworthy, even when the content is identical.

Common triggers include:

- **Cognitive overload** — you're thinking faster than you can speak
- **Anxiety** — nerves tighten your throat and slow your retrieval
- **Habit** — years of using fillers as a verbal security blanket
- **Fear of silence** — treating pauses as awkward gaps that must be filled

Knowing your trigger helps you target the right fix.

---

## 5 Proven Techniques to Stop Saying Um and Uh

### 1. Embrace the Pause

This is the single most effective technique — and the hardest one for most people.

Instead of filling silence with "um," let silence exist. A deliberate pause of one to two seconds sounds far more authoritative than a string of fillers. Think of every great orator: they pause. Pauses signal that you're thinking carefully, not scrambling.

**How to practice:** Record yourself answering a common interview question. Every time you say "um" or "uh," replace it with silence on the next take. It feels awkward at first. Push through it.

### 2. Slow Down Your Speaking Rate

Speed is a filler factory. When you rush, your mouth outruns your brain, and fillers rush in to cover the gap.

Deliberately slowing down by 15–20% gives your brain time to form complete thoughts before your mouth starts moving. You'll naturally reach for fewer fillers because you're no longer scrambling to catch up.

A good target: **120–150 words per minute** for professional presentations. Most people speak at 160–180 when nervous.

**How to practice:** Read a paragraph aloud, time it, then re-read it at a slower pace. Focusing on each word forces your brain to stay ahead of your mouth.

### 3. Prepare Your Transitions

Most fillers cluster at transitions — when you're moving from one idea to the next. If you don't have a bridge ready, your brain stalls and drops an "um."

Memorize a short list of transition phrases:
- "Here's what that means…"
- "Let me give you an example…"
- "The key takeaway is…"
- "What that comes down to is…"

You don't need to use these robotically. Just having them loaded makes it easier to move between ideas without stalling.

### 4. Record and Review Yourself

You can't fix what you can't hear. Most people are shocked the first time they count their own filler words — the number is almost always higher than expected.

Set up your phone and record a two-minute answer to a question you're likely to face. Play it back and tally every "um," "uh," "like," and "you know." Then record again. Repeat three times.

The awareness alone reduces fillers by 30–50% in most people. Your brain starts self-monitoring in real time once it knows the recording is running.

## Building the Long-Term Habit

Eliminating filler words isn't a one-session fix. It's a habit replacement — you're wiring a new default response (pause or transition phrase) over an old one (um/uh). That takes repetition.

A realistic timeline:
- **Week 1–2:** Awareness phase. You start catching yourself in the act.
- **Week 3–4:** Reduction phase. Fillers drop noticeably in prepared contexts.
- **Week 5–8:** Integration phase. The new habit starts carrying over into spontaneous conversation.

Consistency matters more than intensity. Ten minutes every day beats a two-hour session on Sunday.

Track your progress. Celebrate the wins — going from 20 fillers per minute to 5 is a massive improvement, even if it doesn't feel like fluency yet.
